Dad's Solitaire

Setup

Cards are dealt face up into seven columns in the traditional solitaire layout: the first column has one card, the second has two, and so on up to seven. All cards are visible from the start.

The next card from the deck is turned face up and placed above the tableau. This card determines the foundation starting rank and is placed directly onto its suit's foundation pile. The remaining cards form the stock.

Foundations

There are four foundation piles, one for each suit. Each foundation builds upward by suit from the starting rank, wrapping from King back to Ace as needed. For example, if the starting rank is 9, each foundation builds: 9, 10, J, Q, K, A, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8.

The goal is to move all 52 cards onto the foundations.

Moving Cards

  • A single card may be placed on another card of the same colour (red on red, black on black) that is one rank higher. For example, the 5 of Hearts can go on the 6 of Diamonds.
  • A stack of cards may be moved together, but only if every card in the stack is of the same suit and in consecutive ascending order. For example, 5♥ on 6♥ can be moved together onto 7♥ or 7♦, but 5♦ on 6♥ cannot be moved as a pair.
  • Any card or stack may be moved into an empty column.
  • Only the exposed card at the end of a column may be moved to a foundation.

Dealing from the Stock

When you have no more moves to make on the tableau, click the stock pile (or the "Deal to Columns" button) to deal one card from the stock onto the end of each of the seven columns, from left to right. If fewer than seven cards remain in the stock, deal as many as you have.

Controls

  • Click a card to select it (and any same-suit run below it).
  • Click a destination card or empty column to move the selection.
  • Double-click the end card of a column to send it to its foundation.
  • Click the stock pile to deal cards to the columns.
  • Auto moves all possible cards to the foundations at once.
  • Undo (or Ctrl+Z) takes back the last move.
  • Esc deselects the current selection.
